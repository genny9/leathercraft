Leather Crafting
Techniques

There are a number of basic skills in leather working that most projects require, some of these include: saddle stitching, cutting, and edge finishing. 

The following techniques can be applied to something as simple as a keychain or as large as a backpack. Once you are generally familiar with these skills, it is relatively simple to build upon more advanced techniques and elevate them to more advanced projects.
Some of these include: skiving, knife sharpening, and stitching for more unique situations (i.e. box stitch).

Saddle Stitching

Saddle stitching is a very strong hand-sewn stitch, used to join two pieces of leather together. 

This is also the most used and the best way to stitch leather. This style of stitch, done by hand, out performs other stitching methods because it creates a knot at each hole. If the thread breaks somewhere, the whole thing won't unravel. 

Learn more 

Burnishing

Burnishing leather is the method of polishing rough edges. It can be a time-consuming process but results in excellent, clean results. 

Finishing your edges is one of the fastest ways to improve the quality of your work. 
Burnishing finishes an edge by heating loose fibers together through friction.

Learn more 

Dyeing Leather

Leather can be dyed any color, and this process works particularly well on veg-tanned leather.

There is nothing wrong with buying pre-finished leather, but knowing how to dye your own leather is a great skill to have, especially if you want options and cannot afford buying a lot of hides.

Learn more 

Gluing

Gluing leather is generally a straightforward process to fuse multiple leather pieces using glue specifically for leather and suede.  

Leather gluing may sound simple, but doing it well is going to save you a ton of time later on. If you're looking for what kind of glues to use and the best way to keep your edges together for stitching, start here.

Learn more 

Skiving

Skiving leather reduces the thickness of the leather so that you can easily make folds, bends, and hide seams. It generally improves the overall quality and appearance of the leather project. 

Skiving can be difficult, but learning this skill can really improve the look and quality of your work, especially your edges.
